Explaining Health: How AI Explanation Styles and Interaction Modalities Affect User Comprehension of Medical Information
Surya Mani, Aaron Tamte, Lile Zhang
Northeastern University Khoury College of Computer Sciences
{mani.su, tamte.aa, zhang.lile}@northeastern.edu


 Problem Statement
AI is showing up everywhere in healthcare now — patient portals, symptom checkers, and regular chatbots that people use to ask about health stuff. When an AI explains a patient’s lab results or tells them about a diagnosis, the way it explains things matters a lot. If it uses a bunch of medical jargon, the patient might not get it. If it uses simple language and comparisons, they might understand better. But right now, we don’t have solid evidence on which approach actually works better.
There’s also a question about how people interact with the information. Is it better to just read a paragraph of explanation, or is it better to have a conversation where the patient can ask questions? These two things — the style of the explanation and how people interact with it — probably both matter, and they might affect each other. But most existing research only looks at one of them at a time.
The real-world consequences are pretty serious. If someone doesn’t understand their pre-diabetes diagnosis, they might not change their eating habits. If they misread their medication instructions, they could take the wrong amount. And the worst case is when someone thinks they understood everything but actually didn’t — we call this a confidence-comprehension gap. These people won’t go back and ask for help because they don’t realize they need it.
Why This Is Interesting
This project touches on three things that researchers are actively working on. First, there’s explainable AI — the idea that AI should be able to explain its reasoning to people. Second, there’s health literacy. The stats here are pretty bad: only 12% of American adults are considered to have good health literacy, and people forget 40–80% of what a doctor tells them right after the appointment. Third, there’s the idea of using AI to pretend to be patients for testing purposes. If we can get AI personas to behave somewhat like real patients, we could test our systems way faster than running studies with actual people.
Why This Is Hard
There are a few reasons this is hard to do well. First, when we try to simplify medical information, we can accidentally change the meaning. An analogy that makes something easy to understand can also be misleading if it’s not quite right. Second, it’s really hard to tell if someone actually understands something or if they just memorized the right words. A patient who can say “my hemoglobin is low” might have no clue what that means for their health. Third, when a conversation is added instead of just having someone read something, a bunch of things change at once — the person can ask questions, the AI adapts, the whole thing takes longer. So it’s hard to figure out what exactly made the difference. Fourth, getting AI to behave like a real patient is still an open problem. Recent research (Seshadri et al., 2024) shows that simulated users tend to be way too cooperative and don’t make the kinds of mistakes real people make.
Why This Is Important
On the practical side, if AI is going to be how people learn about their health, we need to make sure it actually does a good job explaining things. Bad explanations don’t just confuse people — they can lead to real harm like medication mistakes or missed appointments. On the research side, testing health communication systems with real patients is slow and expensive, and it requires approval from review boards. If we can figure out how to use simulated patients to get useful results, that would speed things up a lot. Our project tries to do both — figure out what kinds of explanations work best for different patients, and also see how far simulation can take us before we need real people.
Design
Hypotheses
H1 (Main Effects): We think simple, analogy-based explanations will lead to better understanding than clinical ones, and that having a conversation will help more than just reading. Both should help on their own, and the best results should come from the combo — simple language plus conversation.
H2 (Dialog Helps More with Hard Language): We think the conversation format will make a bigger difference when the explanation uses clinical jargon. 
H3 (Some Patients Benefit More): We expect the effects to be bigger for certain types of patients. People with low health literacy, high anxiety, and a passive communication style should show the biggest differences across conditions. 
Goal and Success Metrics
The goal is to build a simulation system that can tell us useful things about how different explanation styles and interaction modes affect patient understanding across different kinds of patients.
First, simulation fidelity. The simulated personas need to behave in ways that are at least roughly consistent with what we'd expect from real people. Comprehension scores should correlate with persona health literacy level — low-literacy personas should score lower than high-literacy ones across conditions. We target a Spearman rank correlation of ρ ≥ 0.6 between NVS-based literacy level and mean comprehension score. Personas should also show internal consistency: a persona that struggles with a term in one part of the conversation shouldn't suddenly use it fluently later. We measure this with a manual consistency audit on a 20% sample of transcripts, targeting at least 85% consistency. In the validation study, simulated persona scores should fall within one standard deviation of matched real participant scores on the same comprehension quiz for at least 70% of persona–participant pairs.
Second, hypothesis discrimination. The system needs to produce data with enough variance and structure to actually test our hypotheses. We should observe statistically significant main effects (p < 0.05) for at least one of the two factors on comprehension accuracy, using a two-way repeated-measures ANOVA. Effect sizes (partial η²) should be at least 0.06 (medium effect) for the main effects we hypothesized. For H2, the interaction effect, we need enough statistical power to detect a medium-sized interaction. With 12 personas × 3 scenarios = 36 observations per cell, a sensitivity analysis suggests we can detect effects of f ≈ 0.25 at α = 0.05 and power = 0.80.
Third, judge reliability. The LLM judge's ratings need to be trustworthy. Inter-rater agreement between the LLM judge and a human rater on a 20% sample should reach Cohen's κ ≥ 0.7 (substantial agreement) for comprehension accuracy scoring. The judge should also correctly identify at least 80% of planted comprehension errors in adversarial test cases.
Hard Aspects
The first big challenge is making the fake patients act realistically. A persona with low health literacy needs to struggle with medical words in a believable way, not just get random things wrong. That means the persona prompts need to be really carefully written to set up the right kind of thinking patterns without just scripting the answers.
The second challenge is making sure the AI judge can tell the difference between actually understanding something and just saying the right words. Since both the patient and the judge are language models, there’s a risk that the judge gives high marks to answers that sound smart but are actually wrong.
The third challenge is figuring out what’s actually causing differences in the conversation condition. When a patient can ask questions, a bunch of things change all at once — the conversation is longer, the AI adapts, context builds up. Separating out what matters from what doesn’t is tricky.
Data Collection
For building the scenarios, we need medical reference material from MedlinePlus and clinical guidelines to make sure everything is accurate. For building the personas, we use research on health literacy — especially the NVS test and its scoring system — and studies on how different kinds of patients communicate.
The main data we collect is the full transcript of every conversation between the Explainer and Patient agents, plus the Patient’s quiz answers, satisfaction ratings, and confidence scores. The judge produces scores and written explanations for each rating. For the validation study, we collect the same stuff from real classmates — NVS scores, quiz answers, satisfaction ratings (using the Explanation Satisfaction Scale from Hoffman et al., 2018), and PEMAT understandability scores — and compare them against the matched simulated personas.
Approach
System Architecture
Our system has three main parts: an Explainer Agent, a Patient Agent, and an Interaction Engine that manages the conversation between them. This setup lets us change the explanation style and the interaction mode separately while keeping track of everything that happens.
Explainer Agent
The Explainer Agent is the AI that gives medical explanations. It has a different system prompt for each of the four conditions in our experiment. In Clinical-Direct mode, it talks like a doctor — medical terms, structured format, the kind of language found in a clinical report. In Analogy-Enriched mode, it uses everyday words and comparisons to explain things — like calling red blood cells “oxygen delivery trucks” or describing HbA1c as a “three-month report card for blood sugar.”
In the Static Reading setup, the Explainer just writes out one complete explanation and that’s it. In the Interactive Dialog setup, it gives an initial explanation and then has a back-and-forth conversation with the patient. If the patient seems confused or asks what a word means, the Explainer adjusts. It also throws in comprehension checks during the conversation — like “does that make sense so far?”
Patient Agent
The Patient Agent is an AI that pretends to be a specific kind of patient. Each persona has five traits: health literacy level (based on NVS score ranges), health anxiety (low, medium, or high), whether they have any background knowledge about the topic, their communication style (passive, engaged, or assertive), and basic demographic info like age and education.
A low-literacy, high-anxiety persona might get confused by big medical words, ask worried questions about whether something is really bad, and tend to focus on the scariest possible interpretation. A high-literacy, low-anxiety persona might ask follow-up questions and push back if something doesn’t make sense. The key thing is that the prompt doesn’t tell the patient to get answers right or wrong — it just sets up the kind of person they are, and the comprehension follows from that naturally.
In the Static condition, the Patient reads the explanation and then takes a quiz, rates their satisfaction, and says how confident they are that they understood. In the Dialog condition, they have a full conversation first, then do all the same assessments. Everything stays in character.
Interaction Engine
The Interaction Engine is basically the traffic controller. It sets up each session with the right Explainer prompt and Patient persona, manages whose turn it is to talk in the conversation, and records everything. For dialog sessions, it caps the number of turns so sessions don’t go on forever, but still lets the conversation end naturally when the patient is satisfied. It logs stuff like how long each response is, when the patient asked for something to be re-explained, and when they said they were confused.
Experimental Design
We use a 2×2 setup. One axis is explanation style: Clinical-Direct vs. Analogy-Enriched. The other axis is interaction mode: Static Reading vs. Interactive Dialog. Every persona goes through all four conditions across all three medical scenarios, so we get data for every combination.

Medical Scenarios
We picked three medical scenarios that test different things.
Scenario 1: Blood Test Results (CBC). The patient gets a complete blood count with numbers for white blood cells, red blood cells and a couple other things. One or two values are outside the normal range. This tests whether people can read numbers and understand what “normal range” means. The clinical version shows it like a lab report with medical terms. The analogy version explains each type of blood cell in plain terms — like red blood cells carry oxygen around the body, white blood cells fight off infections — and explains what it means when a number is too high or too low.
Scenario 2: Pre-Diabetes Diagnosis. The patient learns they have pre-diabetes based on their HbA1c number. This tests whether people can understand a condition and a plan for managing it. The clinical version gives the textbook criteria and treatment guidelines. The analogy version explains blood sugar like a thermostat — it should stay in a certain range — and describes HbA1c as a three-month average of blood sugar, kind of like a report card. Lifestyle changes are explained as specific things a person would actually do each day instead of vague medical advice.
Scenario 3: Medication Instructions. The patient gets a prescription with tricky dosing rules — start with one dose, then increase it after two weeks if symptoms haven’t improved enough, and watch for certain side effects that mean the patient should call their doctor. This tests whether people can turn an explanation into the right action. The clinical version uses pharmacy-style language. The analogy version walks through it step by step in plain English.
We generated all the explanations using a large language model with specific prompts for each condition, then checked them against MedlinePlus and clinical guidelines to make sure the medical content was correct. Anything that was wrong or unclear got regenerated with better prompts.
Patient Persona Design
We wanted our fake patients to represent a real range of people, not just one type. So we built each persona along five dimensions: health literacy (low, marginal, or adequate based on NVS scoring), health anxiety (how worried they get about medical stuff), prior knowledge (do they have any personal experience with the condition), communication style (do they ask a lot of questions or just accept what they’re told), and basic demographics (age, education).
Each persona gets a system prompt that describes who they are and how they tend to behave. There’s also a short backstory to make the persona feel more real. The prompt tells the AI to stay consistent with the persona throughout the whole session, including on the quizzes and ratings. But it never tells the AI to get specific questions right or wrong.
Simulation Pipeline
Here’s how the whole simulation runs. For each combination of persona, condition, and scenario, the engine loads up the right Explainer prompt and Patient persona. In the Static condition, the Explainer writes out the explanation and the Patient reads it and responds in character. In the Dialog condition, they have a back-and-forth conversation. After the interaction is done, the Patient — still in character — takes a comprehension quiz, rates how satisfied they were with the explanation (using the Explanation Satisfaction Scale from Hoffman et al., 2018), rates how easy it was to understand (PEMAT items), and says how confident they feel about their understanding.
Then a separate AI judge gets the full conversation transcript, the Patient’s quiz answers, and an answer key we wrote. The judge scores everything on three things: did the patient actually understand the information, how good was the explanation, and (for dialog sessions) how well did the conversation go. With about 10–12 personas, 4 conditions, and 3 scenarios, we end up with around 120–144 sessions total. It’s all automated — no scheduling, no recruitment headaches.
Evaluation
LLM-as-a-Judge Framework
We use a separate LLM instance as an automated judge to evaluate every session. The judge operates independently from both the Explainer and Patient agents — it never participates in the conversation, and it receives the full transcript and quiz responses only after the session is complete. This separation is important because it prevents the judge from being influenced by the flow of conversation in real time.
The judge receives a structured evaluation packet for each session containing: the medical scenario and its reference answer key, the full interaction transcript or the static explanation text, the Patient's quiz answers with the persona's reasoning for each answer, and the Patient's self-reported confidence and satisfaction scores. The judge then produces scores and written justifications across three evaluation dimensions.
Comprehension Accuracy: The judge checks if the patient actually understood the medical information. It works on two levels. First, factual stuff: did the patient get the key facts right? Like which blood values were off, what the HbA1c number means, or when to call the doctor about a side effect. Second, reasoning: did the patient connect the dots? Like understanding why they need to change their diet, or what a low hemoglobin count actually means for how they feel. We wrote an answer key for each scenario that the judge scores against.
Explanation Quality: The judge also rates the Explainer’s performance. Was the explanation clear and well-organized? Did it cover everything important? And was the language level right for the patient? An explanation for A (low literacy, high anxiety) should avoid throwing around medical terms without explaining them. An explanation for B (high literacy, low anxiety) can use some technical words as long as it defines them.
Interaction Quality: For the dialog conditions, the judge looks at how the conversation went. Did the Explainer pick up on when the patient was confused and adjust? Did it actually answer the patient’s questions? Did the conversation flow naturally or feel weird and robotic? The judge also looks for confidence-comprehension gaps — times when the patient said something like “yeah, I get it” but then got a quiz question wrong or said something incorrect later. These gaps are the scariest outcome in health communication, because the patient walks away thinking they’re fine when they’re not.
Judge Calibration
Since the entire evaluation pipeline depends on the LLM judge, we need to make sure it's actually reliable before trusting its scores. We calibrate the judge in three ways.
Adversarial test cases. Before running the full simulation, we create a set of 15–20 hand-crafted test cases designed to trip up a naive judge. These include answers that use correct medical terminology but get the meaning wrong (e.g., "my HbA1c of 6.2 means my blood sugar has been too low for three months"), answers that sound confident and fluent but miss critical safety information (e.g., omitting when to call the doctor about side effects), and answers that are factually correct but show no real understanding — just parroting back phrases from the explanation. We require the judge to correctly identify at least 80% of these planted errors. If it falls below that threshold, we revise the judge's system prompt with more explicit instructions to check for meaning rather than surface fluency, and re-test until it passes.
Human spot-checking. One member of the research team independently scores a stratified random sample of 20% of all sessions, stratified by condition and persona literacy level to ensure coverage. We compute Cohen's κ between the human rater and the LLM judge for each evaluation dimension. Our threshold for acceptable agreement is κ ≥ 0.7. If any dimension falls below κ = 0.6, we analyze the disagreements, identify systematic biases in the judge's scoring, update the judge prompt, and re-score the affected sessions.
Consistency checks. We run the judge twice on a subset of 10% of sessions to check for self-consistency. If the judge gives the same session materially different scores on two runs — defined as a difference of more than 1 point on any 5-point sub-scale, or more than 10 points on the 100-point comprehension score — we flag those cases for human review. High inconsistency (more than 15% of re-scored sessions showing material differences) would indicate the judge prompt needs tightening.

Metrics
We collect and report the following metrics across all sessions.
Primary metrics for hypothesis testing: Comprehension Score on a 0–100 scale, which is the judge's weighted composite of factual recall (60%) and applied reasoning (40%). This is the main dependent variable for testing H1 and H2. Confidence-Comprehension Gap, which is the absolute difference between the patient's self-reported confidence and their actual comprehension score. We report both the mean gap and the proportion of sessions where the gap exceeds 20 points, a threshold we consider clinically meaningful. For H2, we compute the difference-in-differences — how much more dialog helps with clinical language versus analogy-enriched language — and report partial η² from the two-way ANOVA.
Secondary metrics for explanation and interaction quality: Explanation Quality Score on a 1–5 scale, averaging four sub-dimensions — clarity, completeness, structure, and calibration. Interaction Quality Score on a 1–5 scale for dialog sessions only, averaging responsiveness, question handling, comprehension checking, and naturalness. We also report the count of confidence-comprehension gaps flagged per dialog session.
Process metrics for understanding what happened in each session: conversation length in total turns, number of clarification requests from the patient, number of confusion signals (expressed confusion, term misuse, contradictory statements), and number of Explainer adaptations (rephrasing, simplifying, or adding analogies in response to patient behavior).
Persona-level metrics for H3 analysis: comprehension scores broken down by literacy level within each condition, and a benefit delta for each persona — the difference between their best and worst condition. We test H3 by checking whether low-literacy, high-anxiety, passive personas show significantly larger benefit deltas than other persona types.
All metrics are reported with 95% confidence intervals computed via bootstrapping with 10,000 resamples, since our sample sizes are small and we cannot assume normality.

Validation
The simulation is only useful if it bears some resemblance to how real people would respond. We validate in two stages.
Stage 1 is internal validation — simulation sanity checks we run before involving any real participants. We check four things. Literacy ordering: do low-literacy personas consistently score lower than high-literacy personas on comprehension? We test this with a Kruskal-Wallis test across the three literacy levels, expecting p < 0.05. Scenario difficulty: we expect the medication instructions scenario to produce the lowest comprehension scores overall, since it requires translating information into action. If the scenarios don't differ at all, that suggests the personas aren't engaging with the content meaningfully. Dialog should help: if dialog conditions show no improvement over static conditions for any persona type, something is likely wrong with the interaction setup. No ceiling or floor effects: if more than 25% of sessions produce comprehension scores above 95 or below 10, the quiz or the personas need recalibration. If any of these checks fail, we revise the persona prompts, quiz questions, or interaction parameters before proceeding.
Stage 2 is external validation — a human comparison study. We recruit 8–12 classmates to serve as real participants. Each participant takes the Newest Vital Sign health literacy assessment so we can assign them a literacy level, then is randomly assigned to one of the four conditions for each of the three scenarios using a Latin square design to ensure balanced coverage without requiring every participant to do all 12 combinations. They read the explanation or have a conversation with the Explainer via a chat interface, take the same comprehension quiz the simulated patients took, fill out the Explanation Satisfaction Scale and PEMAT understandability items, and report their confidence on the same 0–100 scale.
We then match each real participant to the simulated persona closest to them on literacy level and compare across four dimensions. Comprehension score agreement: we compute the intraclass correlation coefficient between real and simulated scores for matched pairs, targeting ICC ≥ 0.5 (moderate agreement). Rank-order preservation: do the conditions that produce the best comprehension for real participants also produce the best for their matched personas? We test with Kendall's τ on the condition rankings. Directional consistency: at a minimum, we check whether the simulation and the real data agree on the direction of the main effects. If they agree on direction but differ in magnitude, the simulation is still informative for design decisions. Error pattern analysis: we qualitatively compare the types of mistakes real participants make with those made by their matched personas to see if the simulation captures realistic failure modes rather than producing random errors.
We do not expect perfect agreement. The goal is to determine whether the simulation produces patterns that are directionally consistent and useful for identifying which explanation strategies to prioritize in future real-world testing.
Related Work
Explanation Style and Health Literacy
Research on health communication has shown that how something is explained really matters, especially for people who don’t have a lot of health knowledge. Turchioe et al. (2020) found that using visual analogies helps patients understand changes in their health better than showing them graphs. The broader research supports using plain language, teach-back methods (where the doctor asks the patient to repeat back what they learned), and comparisons to everyday things. But nobody has done a controlled comparison of clinical vs. analogy-based AI-generated explanations while also measuring how the interaction mode affects things.
Chatbots in Healthcare
Laranjo et al. (2018) did a big review of conversational agents in healthcare and found that having a back-and-forth conversation can help with engagement and understanding compared to just giving someone information to read. Madumal et al. (2019) came up with a formal framework for how an AI and a user should have a dialog about explanations. The general idea in the literature is that conversation lets people clear up misunderstandings in real time. But most studies don’t separate the effect of the conversation format from the effect of the explanation itself. Our 2×2 design is built to do exactly that.
Using AI to Simulate Participants
Some recent work has tried using LLMs to stand in for real research participants. Aher, Arriaga, and Kalai (2023) showed that LLMs can reproduce some patterns from classic psychology experiments when given the right demographic prompts. But Seshadri et al. (2024) raised some important warnings in a paper called “Lost in Simulation.” They found that simulated participants are less varied in their behavior, make fewer real mistakes, and tend to go along with whatever the system tells them. Nobody has looked at how well LLM personas do specifically for health comprehension tasks, where things like misunderstanding medical terms and health anxiety really matter.
